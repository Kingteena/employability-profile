7. UNIHACK - Hackathon
A hackathon is an experience that a strong applicant for a computer science role might typically talk about. The pressure of designing, building, deploying and testing a viable product in a tight timeframe is immense, and teaches lessons about making sound business plan software desgin. UNIHACK, funded by the European Union and open to all Australian and New Zealand University students, was no exception
UNIHACK, like most hackathons, had us create a piece of software and publish a testable version, as well as pitch it, within 48 hours. Unlike most hackthons, it did not have a specific theme, but instead had several awards, such as "Best Use of Agentic AI", or "Social Impact". I entered the competition with 3 of my friends, and opted to go for the "First-timers" and "Most fun" awards by building a game. Unfortunately, many of us had commitments on day 1, and we realistically only had 24 hours to build this game, which dramatically increased the difficulty of the challenge.
It was a lot of work building a game, and a lot of bugs had to be fixed, but each person leaned heavily into their strengths to produce it. I am quite proud to say that every single thing in the game, from the graphics to the code to even the audio, was made by the team in the span of 24 hours. It was quite difficult, and having to navigate between  the need to sleep and the adrenaline rush of adding new features before the deadline was physically and mentally taxing, but I came out of it proud of the creation we had made.

13. April 14th - Prosperity 
Prosperity 4 was the fourth iteration of an annual Trading competition organised by IMC. As I was engaging with several trading companies, I was wanted to gain insight into what the life of a trader is like, and build these skills in case I wanted to pursue trading. This competition was marketed as a "learn-as-you-go" competition, where teams could come into it as absolute beginners and learn by trial and error. 
My team and I had some experience with marketing making, having attended the Jane Street Electronic Trading Challenge last year. However, I was not prepared for the amount of new information I would have to learn. The challenge was structured into 2 qualifier rounds and 3 final rounds. The qualifier rounds were relatively straightforward with our prior experience, and we were given ample time (3 days) for each round. However, the last few rounds only lasted 48 hours per round, and required us to learn about Options trading and concepts such as the Black-Scholes model to effectively make a competitive trading algorithm. Coming from a pure STEM background, learning about these complex economic theories and implementing them into code a short timeframe was an arduous task. 
Overall, we learnt how to digest unfamiliar domain knowledge and implement it into optimised code, giving us a realistic appreciation of the fast-paced, theory-heavy nature of profitable algorithmic trading.

The people at the stall introduced themselves as members of Black Ochre Data Labs (BODL), a member of the Australian Alliance for Indigenous Genomics (ALIGN). They explained that they work with the Aboriginal Population, and perform genomic analysis to identify certain risks that Aboriginal people are more prone to, and cures that would suit their bodies more. 

### Action
During this discussion, I was able to truly connect with these members and bond with them in a humorous yet professional manner. While they explained their project, I was able to relate some parts of it to my summer research project about cancer evolution, where I worked to simulate single cell RNA data. Despite my background of pure computer science, I decided to pitch my project and what I did, in the hopes of finding any opportunities that might be available with them. I was able to communicate about how I quickly learnt biology concepts and was able to effectively combine it with my computer science knowledge for my previous project. I also conveyed my passion for using my skills to help people. 

### Result
To my surprise, I was met with a lot of support. They were impressed by my work and liked my enthusiasm, and were willing to find opportunities for me. Lisa told me about two other people I should contact, one of them being Stevie Pederson, and invited me to come visit them at their office the following week to talk to them about it in detail. She said that I seemed very personable and would be a great culture fit at BODL. I was overjoyed to hear this; not only was I able to successfully build a network and create connections, but I seemed to have excelled at it, building a genuine human connection and being seen as a possible culture fit. Moreover, I was also able to take another step towards finding my "dream job" by talking to research heads at their office! I was grateful for this opportunity to reach closer to both my goals at the same time. 


### Lesson / Planning
This situation taught me that an opportunity can strike you at any time, even in events which seemingly have nothing to do with your profession, and you just have to take it with open arms. I decided that in the future, I should not be scared to take opportunities, and try regardless. I also learnt how powerful building a genuine human connection could be, and where it can take you; This opportunity might have changed the trajectory of my life, and it would not have happened if I did not genuinely connect with them before pitching myself. It completely transformed my understanding of networking, proving that breaking past the clinical, crowded barriers of standard corporate career fairs through raw, human interaction is often the most effective professional tool available.


## 2. Meeting the BODL Team 

### Situation / Task 
Following up from Kaurna day, I had scheduled a meeting with Stevie Pederson and the BODL team to talk about the projects they were working on and how I could be involved in it. When I met them and the team at the BODL office, I was told about the main project they had in mind for me - PROPHECY. This required processing immense amounts of data concerning type 2 diabetes in Aboriginal populations. The sheer amount of biology keywords and concepts that were thrown around intimidated me, but I had to pitch my side of the story to convince them that I should be involved 

### Action
I made a firm case for how I could be involved, showcasing how I could link my computer science background to handle complex pipelines, and display my shared passion for helping others by utilising my skills. I emphasised my experience and interest in research, and demonstrate my ability to pick up domain knowledge and apply it as shown in my summer research project and trading competitions. When questioned about concepts from Biology I did not know about, or when they explained something I did not have the biology knowledge to understand, I was able to humbly state that I did not know it. 
### Result

They agreed that I could be a good fit for BODL, and were keen to have me on board. They offered a honours project, and mapped out a pathway for me to be involved, with a possible PhD project afterwards. While I was not immediately offered an internship as they did not take unpaid interns, they were keen to have me on board as soon as possible, and I was given a description of the projects I would be working on if funding could be secured. 

They appreciated me being humble enough to admit I did not know a lot of biology, and assured me that I could pick it right before and during the project. I was invited to a seminar called "RAdelaide" that Stevie was conducting about how to use R to process data, and I agreed to level up my skills before starting the project.

 While I had a reassurance that I will gain biology knowledge during my projects, it was already difficult to have an introductory conversation with the team about the work they do because of this gap in my knowledge. As such, I made effort to enrol in MITx: Introduction to Biology (See activity 14), a move that signalled my initiative to the BODL team and strengthen their resolve to get me involved in their projects. 

### Lesson / Planning
This completely removed the career uncertainty I had at the start of the semester. It made me realise a passion I have had for helping people, and helped me see a future where my skills are used towards personalised medicine and uplifting healthcare in disadvantaged communities instead of driving profits as a software engineer. Talking to the team and seeing a day in their life allowed me to put myself in their shoes, assess the culture and help me understand my future

The teams reaction to me enrolling in the MITx course showed me how important understanding your technical gaps are. Sometimes, to overcome your weaknesses, you need to fix them independently through external professional development resources. Doing so requires a lot of discipline and initiative, which is a great signal for your potential employers.

It also reshaped my mindset regarding my career strategy as an international student; by pivoting into highly specialised, niche public health research like bioinformatics, I can align my skills with spaces that evaluate candidates based on unique analytical passion rather than corporate tech visa quotas, giving me a much clearer, more sustainable roadmap for my future in Australia.
